Train/Test Split

#Total sales aggregate
daily_sales <- train %>%
  filter(Open == 1) %>%
  group_by(Date) %>%
  summarise(Sales = sum(Sales)) %>%
  as_tsibble(index = Date)

# Reserve the last 6 weeks as test
cutoff <- max(daily_sales$Date) - 42

train_ts <- daily_sales %>% filter(Date <= cutoff)
test_ts  <- daily_sales %>% filter(Date > cutoff)

nrow(train_ts); nrow(test_ts)
## [1] 900
## [1] 42

##Seasonal naive baseline

naive_fit <- train_ts %>%
  model(SNAIVE(Sales ~ lag("week")))

naive_fc <- naive_fit %>%
  forecast(h = nrow(test_ts))

naive_fc %>%
  autoplot(bind_rows(train_ts, test_ts)) +
  labs(title = "Seasonal Naive Forecast vs Actual")

ETS/ARIMA

fit <- train_ts %>%
  model(
    snaive = SNAIVE(Sales ~ lag("week")),
    ets    = ETS(Sales),
    arima  = ARIMA(Sales)
  )

fc <- fit %>% forecast(h = nrow(test_ts))

fc %>%
  autoplot(bind_rows(train_ts, test_ts), level = NULL) +
  labs(title = "Baseline Models vs Actual")

It is interesting that Sundays have a higher average of sales than the next highest day, Mondays, with no promos being run. However, these results disprove the theory that Monday promos are causing the overforecast because, although there is a slightly higher rate than the other days, the promo rate for Mondays is very similar to the other weekdays. Since Mondays are the second highest days of sales and the models are overforecasting, could the Mondays in the test period just be underperforming compared to the rest of the time period?

Examine Mondays

train %>%
  filter(Open == 1, wday(Date, label = TRUE) == "Mon") %>%
  mutate(period = if_else(Date <= cutoff, "train", "test")) %>%
  group_by(period) %>%
  summarise(AvgMondaySales = mean(Sales), n = n())
## # A tibble: 2 × 3
##   period AvgMondaySales      n
##   <chr>           <dbl>  <int>
## 1 test            8216.   6682
## 2 train           8216. 130878

This theory is also proven incorrect as the test and train Mondays are nearly identical in average sales.

Conclusion

Monday showed the largest and most consistent over-forecasting error across all three baseline models. Two hypotheses were tested and ruled out: elevated Monday promo rates (not present — Monday’s promo rate is in line with other weekdays) and a downward shift in Monday sales between train and test periods (also not present — per-store Monday averages are nearly identical). The most likely explanation is structural: SNAIVE in particular has no smoothing and simply repeats the prior week’s value, so any single volatile week gets propagated forward without correction. This is a known limitation of naive/simple time series methods and motivates moving to a feature-based approach in the next phases of the project.
